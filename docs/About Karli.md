A Note on All Beauty Hair Studio
I actually love this. But first, before I answer the questions, I just want to clarify the whole All Beauty Hair Studio thing. I know it’s been confusing for new clients, which is why I’ve been trying to move away from the All Beauty Hair Studio identity. I used to have my own salon suite. Currently, all of my social media and my website identify me as All Beauty—that’s how people know me, how they tag me, and how they refer to me. I’ve had a hard time letting it go because I also feel like it’s a really good salon name, and what if I open a salon again someday? So that’s how it became “All Beauty Hair Studio, doing business out of The Colour Parlor.” I’m aware it’s still confusing—I just haven’t figured out the workaround yet.
What Made Me Fall in Love with Hair
I’m going to try to make this story short, because it’s quite the love story.
Growing up, I was a singer and a dancer from a really young age. Every year we’d have recitals, concerts, performances, or competitions—all of which required hair and makeup. At a pretty young age, I caught on to how to do my own hair and makeup, and by the time I was a teen, I was helping others with theirs for performances. That led to me doing hair and makeup for friends and family for their special events. I started experimenting with color at around sixteen, which turned into a lot of what I like to call “kitchen hair.”
But back then, my biggest passion was actually makeup. I loved the creativity it gave me. I loved being able to turn someone into a different creature or person. My youngest sister was my muse. She would let me do anything to her. I could go to her room at 2 a.m. and say, “I have an idea—I want to turn you into a fairy, place you in the trees, and take pictures of you for my portfolio.” I’m aging myself here—this was way before the time of social media and online portfolios.
I continued to perform professionally until I was about twenty-two, and then it was time for me to find a “real job.” My family always asked if I wanted to do hair and makeup professionally, and to be honest, at that point it was more of a passion. I thought if I did it for a living, I wouldn’t love it anymore. I struggled with college because I didn’t know I was neurodivergent. School had never been easy for me, but the arts were where my passions lived. I continued to work other jobs while freelancing as a makeup artist, doing lots of wedding and event hair and makeup. Back then, there was a real gray area about licensing for that kind of work. But still, I didn’t think of it as a career. We’re all a little naive when we’re young.
Because of ADHD, OCD, and autoimmune issues, I’ve struggled with my mental health for most of my life. I had a preconceived notion of what success was, and unfortunately, none of those ideas included the arts—even though the arts were one of the only things that ever made perfect sense to me. The arts have a way of making even the worst days look brighter.
At twenty-seven, the worst thing in my life happened to me. I lost my youngest sister—my muse—to a car accident. She was only eighteen and had just graduated high school four months prior. When my muse died, so did my passion for all the arts. I had no desire to sing or dance or do makeup or hair. Life felt pretty dark for a few years. I didn’t go out as much as I used to—not without getting pretty drunk, that is.
During that time, I decided to try going back to school. I also met my husband online. He was just as tech-savvy then as he is now. Just as he’s gotten me interested in AI recently, back then he introduced me to Photoshop. And I started to see a spark of creativity come back. I started looking into classes at school and focused on getting my Associate’s degree in Arts and Visual Communications. I learned everything I could about graphic design. It fed me in the same way hair and makeup did—it was something that allowed me to be fully creative. But my body was failing me in ways I still knew nothing about. Before I was ever diagnosed with hEDS/vEDS, I just thought I was going crazy.
Bas and I got married, and soon after I became pregnant, only to lose our baby twelve weeks later in a public restroom. Pregnancy hormones were a catalyst for the downfall of my body, I like to joke. A year later, I ended up pregnant again. This time I had no real connection to the pregnancy, out of fear we might lose this one too. But we didn’t. Still, pregnancy was incredibly hard on my undiagnosed EDS body. I hated everything about it.
Turns out, after I had my daughter, things didn’t get better. I struggled with really bad postpartum depression. I was having terrible flare-ups all the time and nonstop, scary anxiety attacks. I became agoraphobic and barely left my house for over a year. I ended up having to, once again, seek professional help for my mental health.
And in finding myself and my purpose again, I was reminded of how happy and peaceful the arts had always been for me. I thought back to those times when I felt truly like myself. I was reminded of the happiness my gifts had brought others—how the simplest makeup, when done right, could make someone smile from ear to ear. The way someone looked at themselves when they saw their hair for the first time after a big color change. How moving my body brought life to me, and how music was my soul.
At that point, I knew I had to go back to school again. This time, I was doing it for me—not for someone else, but for me. I could barely leave my house and had no idea how I was going to manage going to school full-time. I decided to take the long route. It was going to take me two years to get my license part-time, but I took it minute by minute, hour by hour, day by day, until I pulled myself out of a very dark hole.
As much as I love being the person who helps change people’s lives, I can never be more grateful than the day I chose myself. Doing hair has changed my life in more ways than I could ever put into words. And every day, when people sit in my chair, I try to give them even a piece of what that means to me.
When a Client Leaves Your Chair and You Know You Nailed It — What Does That Moment Feel Like?
Of course it feels good to nail it, as you put it. And it’s easy to pat yourself on the back in moments like that. But what I love most is the connection. Even better than “nailing it” is when your client looks at themselves and says, “I came in here not even really knowing exactly what I wanted, or how to even explain it to you—but somehow, you nailed it.” That makes me feel amazing, because it means we were able to connect on a level that allowed communication to break through barriers.
The other thing I love is helping my fellow stylists with their business, their formulations, or walking them through a haircut. When they succeed, it feels the same as me succeeding.
I also absolutely love when clients come back to my chair and their hair still looks amazing, and I have to ask them, “So, what are we doing today? Your hair still looks great—what do you feel is missing?” They always laugh and say, “I know! I actually just got a compliment today and I didn’t have the nerve to tell them I haven’t had it done in six months.” That’s priceless to me.
What’s the One Thing You Wish Every New Client Understood About You Before They Even Sat Down?
This one might be one of the hardest to answer. I wish people knew how much I’m in their corner. I’m neurodivergent, and I don’t let that hold me back. In fact, it’s my superpower—I feel like it allows me to see patterns that others might not. I want people to know that I’m an honest hairstylist. I don’t have a problem having the hard conversations. I love educating my clients and helping them make the best choices for them and their lifestyle. Whether it’s clients or other stylists, when I’m working with them, I just want them to know that I’m honest, trustworthy, intentional, and purposeful.
